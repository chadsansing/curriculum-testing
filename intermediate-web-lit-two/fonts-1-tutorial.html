Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. What's webfont?

    TL;DR: A webfont is a font you can call from the Web to work on your webpage.

    A webfont is a 'typeface' like any other font on your computer, except it lives on the Web instead of on your hard drive. Different fonts have different styles. Those styles can communicate feelings, so picking the right webfont is an important design decision for your projects.

    Fonts generally belong to one of several families such as

    • Serif - these fonts are easy to read in print. They have serifs - or 'little feet' - that accent each letter.
    • Sans-serif - these fonts do not have serifs. They are easily readable on the screen.
    • Cursive - these fonts imitate handwriting, but can be difficult to read on devices.
    • Monospace - in these fonts, each letter takes up the same amount of horizontal space. All of the letters take up the equivalent of 'one space' in the font. These fonts are generally used for highlighting parts of texts that should be read like code (but are not part of a page's code), like in a coding tutorial.

    While you could coneivably mix and match fonts throughout a webpage to make sure no two words are alike, the more conservative and unified your design, the fewer fonts you use. People often use more than one font so that they can employ different fonts to show different kinds of information or organizational detail on the screen.

    When you find a webfont to use in a webpage, it will likely be in the form of an external stylesheet or JavaScript script. Both methods - stylesheet and script - reference external links and call webfonts from the Web into your webpage for use. When you use a webfont you are, in effect, calling up its host and asking to borrow it for your page.
